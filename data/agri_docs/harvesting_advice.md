# Maize Harvesting and Post-Harvest Handling

## When to Harvest
Maize is physiologically mature when a black or dark layer forms at the point
where the kernel attaches to the cob (the "black layer"), which typically
occurs when grain moisture is around 30 to 35 percent. For grain maize, allow
the crop to dry further in the field, ideally to 20 to 25 percent moisture,
before harvesting to reduce drying costs, as long as weather and pest
pressure allow it. Delaying harvest too long increases risk of lodging, ear
rot, and losses to birds, rodents, or weevils.

## Harvesting Method
Hand-harvest by snapping ears from the stalk, or mechanically harvest where
equipment is available. Avoid harvesting immediately after heavy rain when
possible, since wet handling increases mold risk. Remove husks promptly
after harvest to allow faster, more even drying.

## Drying
Dry harvested maize to a safe storage moisture content of 13 percent or
below before bagging or bulk storage. Options include:
- Sun-drying on a raised, well-ventilated platform or tarpaulin, turning
  regularly, protected from rain and direct ground contact.
- Mechanical or solar dryers where available, which reduce weather risk and
  drying time significantly compared to open sun-drying.
A simple field check: dry kernels should be hard and break with a sharp
crack rather than bending or squashing, and shelled grain should rattle
loosely, not feel soft or rubbery.

## Shelling and Cleaning
Shell (remove kernels from the cob) after drying, either by hand or with a
sheller, and clean out broken kernels, cob fragments, and debris before
storage, since damaged kernels and debris both accelerate mold and pest
buildup in storage.

## Storage
Store dried, cleaned grain in a cool, dry, well-ventilated space, off the
ground and away from walls to reduce moisture uptake and pest access.
Hermetic (airtight) storage bags are strongly recommended where available,
since they starve storage pests of oxygen without needing chemical
treatment. Inspect stored grain periodically for signs of moisture, mold
smell, or pest activity, and re-dry or treat promptly if problems are found.

## Reducing Post-Harvest Loss
Post-harvest losses from poor drying, pests, and mold commonly account for a
significant share of total smallholder maize losses, in many cases more than
losses from field pests or disease. Investing in proper drying and hermetic
storage is often one of the highest-return practices a smallholder farmer can
adopt, since it protects grain that has already been grown rather than
requiring additional inputs.
