# Maize Fertilizer Recommendations

## General Principles
Maize has high nitrogen demand, moderate phosphorus demand (especially early
in growth for root development), and moderate potassium demand. Exact rates
depend on existing soil fertility, so a soil test is the most reliable guide
where available. In its absence, the general guidance below is a reasonable
starting point for average-fertility smallholder soils.

## Basal (Planting-Time) Application
Apply a compound fertilizer with both phosphorus and nitrogen (such as a
NPK 10-20-10 or similar compound blend) at planting, placed a few centimeters
to the side and below the seed, not in direct contact with it. A commonly
used smallholder rate is approximately 100 to 150 kg per hectare of a
compound NPK blend, adjusted for known soil fertility and crop budget.
Phosphorus is especially important at this stage since it supports early
root establishment, which the plant cannot make up for later in the season.

## Top-Dressing (Nitrogen)
Apply a nitrogen-rich top-dressing fertilizer (such as urea, 46 percent
nitrogen, or calcium ammonium nitrate) in two split applications:
1. First top-dressing: 3 to 4 weeks after emergence, when the plant is at
   roughly knee height, applying around 50 kg per hectare of urea.
2. Second top-dressing: at or just before tasseling (roughly 7 to 8 weeks
   after emergence), applying a similar or slightly reduced rate, since this
   is when nitrogen demand peaks for grain fill.
Apply top-dressing fertilizer when soil is moist (just after rain, or before
expected rain) and keep it away from direct contact with leaves to avoid
scorch. Avoid applying nitrogen when the field is waterlogged, since a large
share can be lost to leaching or denitrification.

## Organic Matter and Soil Health
Where available, well-decomposed manure or compost applied before planting
(commonly 5 to 10 tonnes per hectare) improves soil structure and nutrient
holding capacity, and can reduce the amount of mineral fertilizer needed over
time. Incorporating crop residue and legume rotations also builds long-term
soil fertility and reduces total fertilizer cost.

## Low-Budget Adjustment
On very limited budgets, prioritize a smaller, well-placed basal phosphorus
application over a larger nitrogen-only application, since poor early root
development cannot be corrected later. If only one top-dressing is
affordable, apply it around 4 to 5 weeks after emergence rather than waiting
until tasseling, since vegetative nitrogen deficiency early in the season is
usually more yield-limiting than a slightly reduced late-season rate.

## Warning Signs of Deficiency
- Nitrogen deficiency: older, lower leaves turn pale yellow starting at the
  tip in a V-shaped pattern along the midrib.
- Phosphorus deficiency: stunted plants with a purplish tinge on leaves,
  especially in cool soil conditions early in the season.
- Potassium deficiency: yellowing and browning (scorch) along the margins of
  older leaves, with weak stalks prone to lodging.
