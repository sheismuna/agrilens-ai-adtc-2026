# Maize Pest Management

## Fall Armyworm
Symptoms: ragged, elongated holes in leaves, especially in the whorl; sawdust-
like frass (droppings) visible in the funnel of the plant; larvae are
greenish to brown with a distinctive inverted "Y" mark on the head capsule.
Management: Scout fields weekly from emergence, focusing on the whorl.
Hand-pick and destroy egg masses and young larvae where infestation is low.
Apply an approved insecticide (such as one containing emamectin benzoate or
a suitable biopesticide like Bacillus thuringiensis) directly into the whorl
when larvae are small, since larger larvae hide deep in the whorl and are
harder to control. Intercropping with legumes and maintaining natural
predator habitat (birds, parasitic wasps) can reduce pressure over time.

## Stem Borer
Symptoms: small round holes in the stem, "dead heart" (dead central whorl
leaf) in young plants, and tunneling inside the stalk that weakens the plant
and can cause lodging.
Management: Destroy old maize stalks after harvest, since larvae overwinter
inside them. Plant early to avoid peak borer emergence. Use resistant
varieties where available. Apply insecticide granules into the whorl at
early infestation if damage exceeds economic threshold (commonly cited as
around 10 percent of plants showing dead heart symptoms).

## Termites
Symptoms: hollowed out stems near the soil line, wilting and lodging of
otherwise healthy-looking plants, visible mud tubes on the stem base.
Management: Avoid leaving un-decomposed organic matter or old stumps in the
field, since these attract termite colonies. Treat seed or soil with a
recommended termiticide in known high-pressure fields. Avoid excessive
drought stress where possible, since stressed plants are more attractive to
termite attack.

## Aphids
Symptoms: clusters of small, soft-bodied insects (green, yellow, or black)
on the underside of leaves and around the tassel, causing curling and
stunted growth, and excreting sticky honeydew that promotes sooty mold.
Management: Natural predators such as ladybird beetles usually keep aphid
populations under control. Treat with an appropriate insecticidal soap or
recommended systemic insecticide only if populations are heavy and natural
predators are absent, since aphids are also the main vector for maize lethal
necrosis co-infection.

## Storage Weevils (Post-Harvest)
Symptoms: small holes bored into stored grain, powdery residue, and
significant weight loss in stored maize over a few months.
Management: Dry grain to below 13 percent moisture before storage. Use
hermetic storage bags (triple-layer or equivalent) to exclude oxygen and
prevent weevil development. Clean storage containers thoroughly between
seasons. Where available, an approved storage-grain protectant dust can be
mixed into grain prior to bagging.
