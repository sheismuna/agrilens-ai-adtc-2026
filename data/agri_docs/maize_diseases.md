# Common Maize Diseases: Identification and Treatment

## Northern Corn Leaf Blight
Symptoms: long, grey-green, water-soaked, cigar-shaped lesions on lower leaves
first, spreading upward. Lesions dry to a tan color and can merge to kill
large sections of leaf tissue. Favored by cool, humid weather and continuous
maize cropping without rotation.
Treatment: Remove and destroy infected crop residue after harvest. Rotate
maize with a non-host crop such as legumes for at least one season. Plant
resistant or tolerant maize varieties where available. In severe outbreaks, a
foliar fungicide containing a triazole or strobilurin active ingredient can
be applied at first sign of disease, following the product label rate.

## Maize Streak Virus
Symptoms: narrow, broken, pale yellow to white streaks running parallel to
the leaf veins, most visible on young leaves. Severely infected plants are
stunted and produce small or no cobs. Spread by leafhoppers, not seed-borne.
Treatment: There is no chemical cure once a plant is infected. Control the
leafhopper vector early in the season using an appropriate systemic
insecticide seed treatment or early foliar spray. Remove and destroy severely
infected plants to reduce virus source. Plant streak-tolerant varieties in
high-pressure areas, and plant early in the season to avoid peak leafhopper
populations.

## Gray Leaf Spot
Symptoms: small, rectangular, tan to gray lesions bound by leaf veins,
usually starting mid-canopy and progressing upward under humid conditions.
Lesions can coalesce and cause premature leaf death.
Treatment: Rotate away from maize for at least one season where the disease
has been severe. Reduce residue-borne inoculum through tillage or residue
management if erosion risk allows. Use resistant hybrids. Fungicide
application is rarely justified for smallholder plots except in high-value
seed production fields with a history of severe disease.

## Maize Lethal Necrosis
Symptoms: a combination of chlorotic mottling, leaf necrosis starting at the
leaf margins, and severe stunting, caused by co-infection of maize chlorotic
mottle virus and a cereal potyvirus, both spread by insects and sometimes
seed. Can cause complete crop loss in severe cases.
Treatment: There is no cure once infected. Use certified, disease-free seed.
Control aphid and thrips vectors with recommended insecticides. Rogue
(remove) infected plants early. Avoid planting maize next to previously
infected fields, and practice crop rotation with non-cereal crops.

## Common Rust
Symptoms: small, round to elongate, reddish-brown pustules scattered on both
leaf surfaces, which can turn dark brown to black as the plant matures.
Treatment: Usually not economically damaging in most smallholder settings
unless infection is very heavy early in the season. Resistant hybrids are the
most practical control. Fungicide is rarely needed except in seed production
or highly susceptible varieties under prolonged wet weather.
